# Alert Investigation: Traffic Spike Detected

## Timestamp
2026-03-31 ~14:57-14:59

---

## Summary

An alert was generated indicating a significant spike in network event volume within a short time window. The spike exceeded the defined threshold for normal activity. Splunk investigation was performed to identify the source of the spike, analyze traffic patterns, and determine whether the activity was malicious.

---

## Network Details

- Time Window:
  - 14:57 -> 149 events
  - 14:58 -> 752 events
  - 14:59 -> 253 events
- Top Source IPs:
  - 192.168.1.26 -> 703 events
  - 192.168.1.13 -> 390 events
- Additional Sources: Multiple IPv4 and IPv6 with lower event counts
- Protocol: Mixed
- Traffic Type: Internal network activity

---

## Investigation Process

1. Alert identified in Splunk SIEM
2. Reviewed time-based spike using:
  index=main | timechart span=1m count | where count > 100
3. Identified a sharp increase in event volume at 14:58
4. Ran query to determine source contribution:
  index=main earliest=-10m latest=now | stats count by src_ip | sort -count
5. Identified top contributing source IPs responsible for the spike
6. Reviewed distribution of traffic across additional IP addresses
7. Evaluated whether the activity aligned with normal or malicious behavior

---

## Findings

- A significant spike in event volume was observed at 14:58 with 752 events
- The majority of traffic originated from internal IP addresses:
  - 192.168.1.26 generated 703 events
  - 192.168.1.13 generated 390 events
- Additional lower-volume traffic was observed from multiple IPv4 and IPv6 addresses
- No single external source dominated the traffic
- The spike was short in duration and not sustained over time
- No evidence of repeated attack patterns or coordinated malicious activity

---

## Analysis

- The spike was primarily driven by internal hosts rather than an external source
- The presence of multiple contributing IPs indicates normal network activity rather than targeted behavior
- The lack of persistence and rapid decline in event volume suggests a short-duration burst of activity
- No indicators of scanning, exploitation attempts, or malicious payload delivery were identified
- The observed pattern is consistent with legitimate user or system-generated network activity

---

## Security Context

- Internal systems can generate high volumes of traffic during normal operation
- Multiple devices contributing to a spike is typical in active network environments
- Mixed IPv4 and IPv6 traffic further supports normal network behavior
- Volume-based detection rules may trigger alerts during legitimate activity spikes
- Proper context and source analysis are required to distinguish benign activity from potential threats

---

## Risk Assessment

Severity: Low
  - Traffic originated primarily from internal systems
  - No evidence of malicious intent
  - No indicators of compromise
  - Activity consistent with expected network usage patterns

---

## Conclusion

The alert was triggered due to a short-duration spike in network event volume, primarily generated by internal hosts. Analysis showed that the majority of traffic originated from two internal IP addresses, with additional contributions from other network devices.

The observed activity was not consistent with malicious behavior and is best classified as legitimate network usage. This event highlights the importance of correlating volume-based alerts with source attribution to avoid false positives.

---

## Tools Used

- Splunk (SIEM)
- Suricata (IDS)

---

## Key Takeaways

- Traffic spikes should be analyzed at the source level to determine origin
- Internal systems can generate high event volumes during normal operation
- Short-duration spikes do not necessarily indicate malicious activity
- Volume-based alerts require contextual validation to reduce false positives
- Correlating event volume with source IPs improves investigation accuracy
